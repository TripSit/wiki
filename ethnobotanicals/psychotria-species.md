| **Family**<br><br>Rubiaceae (Coffee Family)<br><br>Most of the approximately 1,200 to 1,400<br><br>Psychotria species that have been described are<br><br>found in the tropical zones of Central and South<br><br>America, although a few species occur in the rain<br><br>forests of Malaysia and in New Caledonia<br><br>(Standley 1930). In the Caribbean, the seeds of<br><br>some species, e.g. Psychotria nervosa, are referred<br><br>to as wild coffee and drunk as a coffee substitute<br><br>(cf. Coffea arabica). The fruits of many Psychotria<br><br>species (P. involucrata Swartz, P. nudiceps Standley)<br><br>are regarded as poisonous (Schultes 1969, 158;<br><br>1985). N,N-DMT has been demonstrated to be<br><br>present in several species. Some contain the<br><br>alkaloid psychotridine, and others indoles (Lajis et<br><br>a1. 1993). Some species (Psychotria poeppigiana<br><br>Muel1. Arg., Psychotria ulviformes Sterm.) appear<br><br>to contain opium-like constituents (Elisabetskyet<br><br>a1. 1995, 78). The Yucatec Maya regard the Central<br><br>American species Psychotria acuminata Benth. (ixanal)<br><br>and Psychotria tenuifolia Sw. (x'anal) as<br><br>«male" and «female" counterparts and use them to<br><br>treat nervousness and sleeplessness (Arvigo and<br><br>Balick 1994, 45, 105*). In Europe, Psychotria<br><br>emetica (1. fi1.) Mutis, the Peruvian vomit plant,<br><br>was known in particular as a counterfeit for ipecac<br><br>(Cephaelis ipecacuanha [Brot.] Tussac [syn.<br><br>Psychotria ipecacuanha (Brot.) Stokes]) (Ratsch<br><br>1991a, 136f.*; Schneider 1974, 3:135f.*). The<br><br>vomit-inducing substance emetine is said to occur<br><br>in numerous Psychotria species (Fisher 1973, 231).<br><br>**Psychotria brachypoda (Muell. Arg.) Britton**<br><br>This Psychotria is used traditionally as a pain<br><br>medicine. The species contains active constituents<br><br>with opium-like, analgesic effects (Elisabetsky et<br><br>a1. 1995).<br><br>**Psychotria carthaginensis Jacquin-sameruca**<br><br>According to information provided by the<br><br>Colombian Makuna Indians, eating the fruit of<br><br>this bush will induce perceptual alterations that<br><br>can persist for days, nausea, weakness, and fever<br><br>(Schultes 1969, 158). The leaves, which contain<br><br>some N,N-DMT, are used as an ayahuasca additive<br><br>(Schultes 1985, 118).<br><br>**Psychotria colorata (Willd. ex R. et S.) Muell. Arg.**<br><br>This bush is known as perpetua do mato in the<br><br>Brazilian Amazon, where it is used in folk medicine<br><br>to treat ear and lower abdominal pain. The<br><br>Caboclos produce eardrops by heating the flowers<br><br>in banana leaves on hot ashes. A decoction of the<br><br>roots and fruits is drunk to treat abdominal pains.<br><br>The leaves and flowers have been found to<br><br>contain alkaloids with opium-like effects whose<br><br>structures have not yet been determined<br><br>(Elisabetsky et a1. 1995). | **Psychotria poeppigiana Muel!. Arg.-oreja del<br><br>diablo (Spanish, "clevil's ear")**<br><br>In Amazonia (Ecuador), the nectar of this species<br><br>is used as a traditional ear medicine. The leaves are<br><br>rich in N,N-DMT and are evidently well suited for<br><br>use as an ayahuasca additive (ayahuasca analogs)<br><br>(Rob Montgomery, pers. comm.). In the Putumayo<br><br>region of Colombia, the roots are used to treat<br><br>lung ailments (Schultes 1985, 119; Schultes and<br><br>Raffauf 1990, 395*).<br><br>Among the Ka'apor, Psychotria poeppigiana<br><br>Muel1. Arg. is called yawaru-ka'a, «black jaguar<br><br>plant;' or tapi'i-ka'a, «tapir plant" (Balee 1994,<br><br>303*). These names suggest that the plant may<br><br>be used for shamanic purposes (animal<br><br>transformation) .<br><br>**Psychotria psychotriaefolia (Seem.) Standley**<br><br>In the Colombian Putumayo region, the leaves of<br><br>this species are used together with Banisteriopsis<br><br>caapi to produce ayahuasca. In Ecuador, both the leaves and the fruits are used for this purpose<br><br>(Schultes 1969, 158). The addition of this plant to<br><br>the mixture is said to deepen and prolong the<br><br>visions. The leaves contain N,N-DMT. The Kofan<br><br>Indians call the plant oprito. They use this same<br><br>name to refer to the "heavenly people" that they<br><br>contact while under the influence of ayahuasca<br><br>(164). This species may be synonymous with<br><br>Psychotria viridis.<br><br>**Psychotria spp.**<br><br>Among the many members of the genus<br><br>Psychotria, there are certainly other species that<br><br>contain N,N-DMT and may be suitable for use as<br><br>ayahuasca additives.
